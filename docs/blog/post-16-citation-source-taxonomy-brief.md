## Angle lock

**This post is descriptive taxonomy only.** It names the twelve surface categories AI synthesizes from when answering candidate-intent queries about employers and shows what each category is, where it shows up, and why it earns its own line in the taxonomy. It does **not** introduce scoring, depth tiers, anchored/present/thin/absent labels, or any prescriptive frame about whether a company is "deep enough" on a given surface.

Authority depth — the scored interpretation layer on top of this taxonomy — is the subject of later posts (sequenced for #22, #25, and #29 in the LinkedIn cadence; full long-form treatment held until the H1 2026 benchmark on June 30 and a Q3 follow-up post). Post 16 establishes the **noun** first. The vocabulary has to exist in the discourse before the measurement can land.

**What the reader walks away with:** a map of the twelve surfaces AI is actually reading when a candidate asks where to work, and a structured way to think about what kind of surface each one is. Not a scorecard. Not a remediation plan. A taxonomy.

**Out of scope for this post (captured in Citation Source Inventory, not Authority Surface Map):**

- Wikipedia, Crunchbase, ZoomInfo, and other reference sites — these are reference surfaces, not surfaces a company can deliberately move through investment. Post 16 explicitly names them as cited *somewhere* (Citation Source Inventory) but excluded from the Authority Surface Map for design reasons.
- Paid placements (sponsored LinkedIn job posts, sponsored Indeed listings) — placement, not synthesis.
- Private communities (Slack, Discord, internal forums) — AI cannot read them.
- Podcasts/video without transcripts — not synthesizable, so not in the corpus.

## Section-by-section outline

### Section 1: Why a taxonomy (≈300 words)

**Hook:** When AI answers a candidate's question about working at a named company, it synthesizes from a specific set of public surfaces. That set is smaller than most employer brand teams expect and structured differently than the surfaces most teams actively manage.

**The lift:** Frame this post as the missing vocabulary. There is no industry-standard category list for "the surfaces AI reads when recommending employers." Post 16 publishes one. Twelve categories, grouped into five families.

**Compliance lock:**

- No comparison to other audit categories or vendor categories. The taxonomy stands on its own.
- No "your team is missing something" framing. The post tells employer brand teams **what the surfaces are**, not what they're doing wrong.
- The five families and twelve categories appear here in summary, then expand in subsequent sections.

**One-line preview of the structure:**

> Employer-owned (4): Careers Site · Company Newsroom/Blog · Functional Team Content · Leadership Social Content
> Peer-reviewed (3): Glassdoor · Generalist Peer Review · Sector-Specific Peer Review
> Specialist (3): Compensation Specialist Platforms · Persona-Specific Community Platforms · Reddit & General Professional Forums
> Independent (1): Independent Voice Content
> Authoritative (1): Trade & Business Press

### Section 2: How this taxonomy was built (≈250 words)

**Methodological note** — short, honest, no overclaim.

- Built from the AI-cited surfaces observed in the scored corpus across Diagnostics and pre-sprint research scans (April–May 2026).
- Surfaces that appear repeatedly across candidate-intent queries and across multiple models earn category status.
- Reference surfaces (Wikipedia, Crunchbase, ZoomInfo) are cited but routed to a separate inventory rather than the Map because they are not surfaces a company can deliberately strengthen through investment. The Map is for surfaces where deliberate work moves the citation.
- The line between "category" and "example surface" is drawn at the level of editorial control and audience tailoring. Glassdoor earns its own line because the dominant single citation source across sectors deserves its own measurement scope; Built In and Vault sit inside Sector-Specific Peer Review because the editorial pattern is the same across sector-tailored platforms.

**Canonical phrasing rule introduced once and used throughout:** the post discusses authority and citation "across the AI-cited candidate-intent surfaces in the scored corpus" — not "across the web." Establishes the scoping discipline that later posts will inherit.

### Section 3: Employer-owned surfaces (≈350 words)

The four surfaces a company directly publishes on its own domain or under its own named authorship.

| # | Category | What it is | Worked example (sector rotation) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Careers Site** | The company-owned recruiting surface — careers.[company].com, jobs.[company].com, /careers, employee profile pages on the company domain | A regional health system that publishes nurse-leader profiles on /careers — AI cites the leader profiles when candidates ask about clinical leadership culture |
| 2 | **Company Newsroom/Blog** | Press releases, /newsroom, /blog, /insights, leadership announcements, values content | A multi-state credit union — the newsroom carries the M&A announcement that AI cites when candidates ask whether the company is stable |
| 3 | **Functional Team Content** | Team-specific public content on the company-owned domain — engineering blog, sales playbook, design blog, customer success learning content, operations/safety content | A national logistics company publishes a fleet-safety operating blog — AI cites it when operations candidates ask about safety culture; sector-agnostic by design |
| 4 | **Leadership Social Content** | Public content authored by named company leaders on non-company-owned platforms — LinkedIn executive posts, X posts, leadership-authored Medium/Substack, named-leader YouTube, founder podcast appearances | A mid-market industrial distributor — the CFO's LinkedIn essays on inventory operating discipline get cited when finance candidates ask about the company's operating philosophy |

**Editorial note for the section:** worked examples deliberately span health, financial services, logistics, and industrial distribution. The taxonomy is sector-agnostic. Functional Team Content is the line that does the most work here — engineering blogs are not the only thing that fits.

### Section 4: Peer-reviewed surfaces (≈300 words)

The three surfaces where employees write the citations.

| # | Category | What it is | Worked example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | **Glassdoor** | Singleton category. Dominant single citation source across sectors, scored separately to preserve signal | Across the scored corpus, Glassdoor citations appeared in roughly 7 of every 10 candidate-intent queries that surfaced peer-review content for the named company |
| 6 | **Generalist Peer Review** | Cross-sector employee review platforms — Comparably, Indeed reviews, Kununu (European markets) | A specialty retailer — AI cited Comparably's leadership rating page when candidates asked about management style for a customer experience role |
| 7 | **Sector-Specific Peer Review** | Sector- or persona-tailored review platforms — Built In (tech), Vault (finance, consulting, law), sector-tailored review platforms | A regional investment bank — AI cited Vault's compensation and culture review when finance candidates asked about deal team experience |

**Editorial note:** Glassdoor gets its own line not because Antellion thinks it's the most important surface but because it is statistically the dominant single citation source across the scored corpus and rolling it into "peer review" would erase signal. Methodological honesty, not vendor endorsement.

### Section 5: Specialist surfaces (≈350 words)

The three surfaces tailored to specific candidate communities. Highest-leverage section in the taxonomy because these are the surfaces most employer brand teams have the thinnest visibility into.

| # | Category | What it is | Worked example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8 | **Compensation Specialist Platforms** | Role-specific compensation data surfaces with active community contribution — Levels.fyi, RepVue, salary.com community boards | A mid-market enterprise SaaS — AI cited RepVue's sales-org compensation transparency rating when revenue candidates asked about commission structure |
| 9 | **Persona-Specific Community Platforms** | Named professional communities and forums tied to specific candidate personas — Blind, Gainsight Community, Customer Success Network, Pavilion, SHRM communities | A late-stage SaaS — AI cited a Gainsight Community thread on team structure when senior customer success candidates asked about scope of role |
| 10 | **Reddit & General Professional Forums** | Public professional discussion communities not tied to a single persona platform — Reddit (r/sales, r/customersuccess, r/managers, sector-specific subreddits), Hacker News, Lobsters | A regional bank — AI cited a r/financialcareers thread on hiring process speed when finance candidates asked about the interview experience |

**Editorial line:** these three surfaces are where AI synthesis most often surfaces detail that the company hasn't authored and may not realize is being read. Not a critique of the company; a fact about how AI assembles answers for persona-coded candidate questions.

### Section 6: Independent voice (≈200 words)

| # | Category | What it is | Worked example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | **Independent Voice Content** | Named-author independent content surfaces not on company-owned domains — Substack newsletters (non-leadership authors), podcast transcripts, conference talk transcripts, named-author Medium, named-author YouTube | A health insurance carrier — AI cited a named industry-analyst Substack essay when actuarial candidates asked about the company's product strategy |

**Editorial note:** Independent Voice Content is one category, not five, because the editorial pattern is the same: a named human author publishes on a surface they control, AI synthesizes the content. The platform varies; the citation behavior does not.

### Section 7: Authoritative press (≈200 words)

| # | Category | What it is | Worked example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | **Trade & Business Press** | Editorial press surfaces — Bloomberg, Reuters, WSJ, FT, HR trade publications (TLNT, HR Brew, Recruiting Daily, SHRM articles), sector trade press, regional business journals | A specialty chemicals manufacturer — AI cited a regional business journal acquisition story when operations candidates asked about company growth trajectory |

**Editorial note:** trade press behaves differently from general business press but the editorial-authority signal is the same. They share a category.

### Section 8: What's *not* in the taxonomy and why (≈250 words)

The honest disclosure section.

- **Reference sites** (Wikipedia, Crunchbase, ZoomInfo): AI cites them constantly. They are inventoried in the Citation Source Inventory section of a Diagnostic Report. They are excluded from the Authority Surface Map because a company cannot meaningfully move a Wikipedia article through its own work; the surface is descriptive, not actionable. Including reference sites in the Map would dilute the signal of surfaces a company can actually invest in.
- **Paid placement** (sponsored LinkedIn job posts, sponsored Indeed listings, paid Reddit promotions): placement, not synthesis surface. AI rarely cites them in candidate-intent answers.
- **Private communities** (Slack, Discord, internal forums): AI cannot read them.
- **Podcasts/video without transcripts**: if AI cannot synthesize, the surface is not in the corpus. Podcast and conference talks *with* transcripts route to #11.

**Editorial line:** the Map is intentionally scoped to actionable authority depth — surfaces where a company can move the score through deliberate work. Reference and paid surfaces are real, cited, and tracked in inventory; they are simply not where the deliberate-investment conversation lives.

### Section 9: What this taxonomy is for (≈200 words)

**Close.** This is not "the surfaces you should publish on." It is "the surfaces AI is actually reading when a candidate asks about your company." Knowing the difference is the first move.

The taxonomy will be the structural backbone of one section of the H1 2026 benchmark publishing June 30. That section will look at surface-distribution patterns in aggregate across companies — not authority scoring of any individual company.
