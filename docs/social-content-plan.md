## Part 2: TikTok / Instagram Short-Form Video Plan

### Audience Reality Check (Read This First)

Before investing time in short-form video, the honest assessment of whether the target audience is reachable on these platforms.

**Who is the buyer?** Chief Talent Officers, VP Talent Acquisition, Head of Employer Brand, CPOs at companies with 2,000-10,000 employees. These are mid-career to senior professionals, typically ages 35-55.

**Are they on TikTok?**

Mostly no -- not in a professional capacity. TikTok's user demographics skew younger (18-34 is the core), and even TikTok's growing older demographic (35-49) is using the platform for entertainment, not for evaluating enterprise tools or following B2B thought leadership. A VP of Talent Acquisition may have a personal TikTok account for recipes and travel videos. They are not scrolling TikTok looking for AI employer visibility insights.

The exception: employer brand professionals in their late 20s to mid-30s who are closer to the practitioner level. These are not the decision-makers, but they are the internal champions who flag interesting ideas to their VP. They are on TikTok. They follow "work culture" and "HR TikTok" content. But they are recommenders, not buyers.

**Are they on Instagram?**

More likely than TikTok, but still primarily personal use. Instagram Reels reach a broader age demographic than TikTok, and some B2B creators have built audiences there (particularly in marketing, consulting, and leadership development). But the engagement patterns are casual. A CPO might see a Reel in their feed, but the path from "interesting 30-second video" to "I should evaluate this vendor" is much longer than on LinkedIn.

**Where is the actual buyer attention?**

LinkedIn. Overwhelmingly. This is where VP TAs read, engage, share, and discover new vendors. The problem is that Jordan cannot use LinkedIn as an Antellion channel. Which is why short-form video is being considered in the first place -- it is a channel where the LinkedIn constraint does not apply.

**The honest conclusion:**

Short-form video on TikTok and Instagram will NOT directly reach the primary buyers. It can reach:
1. Junior-to-mid employer brand practitioners who may surface the concept to their VP
2. The broader "future of work" audience who amplify ideas that eventually reach enterprise buyers through other channels
3. Journalists and content creators who cover HR/talent trends and might cite or reference the findings
4. AI models that index video transcripts and cross-platform content (a secondary GEO benefit)

The value of short-form video for Antellion is not direct lead generation. It is ambient awareness and content amplification that supports the cold email and blog strategy. If Jordan expects to generate leads directly from TikTok, the answer is: that will almost certainly not happen. If Jordan expects short-form video to create a wider surface area for the ideas -- making it more likely that a cold email recipient has already encountered the concept somewhere -- then it has value, but it is lower priority than blog writing, email operations, and LinkedIn.

**Recommendation:** Do short-form video ONLY if Jordan genuinely enjoys the format and can batch-produce it efficiently. Do not do it as a grind. The ROI per hour is lower than blog writing or LinkedIn posting for this specific ICP. If the choice is "write one more excellent blog post" or "record three TikTok videos," the blog post wins every time for this audience.

With that context established, here is the plan for those who proceed.

### 1. Content Format Options for This Niche (Ranked)

Ranked by the combination of (a) likely engagement on TikTok/Instagram and (b) credibility with the secondary audience that might surface this content to enterprise buyers.

**Tier 1: High Engagement + High Credibility**

1. **Screen recording showing real AI responses** -- The single strongest format for this content. Record ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity responding to a candidate-intent query ("What are the best fintech companies to work for in NYC?") and react to what appears. The audience sees the proof in real time. This format works on both platforms and feels native to the "I tested this and here is what happened" genre that performs well on TikTok.

2. **"Did you know?" format with data overlay** -- Text on screen with a specific, surprising data point. "Did you know that when candidates ask AI for the best companies to work for, 60% of mid-market companies are never mentioned?" Voiceover explains why. Short (15-30 seconds), shareable, and the data point does the heavy lifting.

**Tier 2: Good Engagement + Good Credibility**

3. **Talking head (Jordan on camera sharing a single finding)** -- Works if Jordan is natural on camera. The "one surprising thing I learned" format is proven on both platforms. The risk: talking-head B2B content on TikTok can feel out of place unless the delivery is conversational and the insight is genuinely surprising. For Instagram Reels, this format works better because the audience expectation is broader.

4. **Myth-busting format** -- "Most companies think having a good Glassdoor score means AI will recommend them. I tested this. Here is what actually happens." Strong hook structure, clear narrative arc, and the contrarian angle is built in. Works well on both platforms.

**Tier 3: Moderate Engagement + High Credibility (But Lower Reach)**

5. **Text-on-screen with voiceover** -- Professional-looking, easy to produce, good for conveying complex ideas. But it lacks the human element that drives engagement on TikTok specifically. Better for Instagram Reels where the audience tolerates more polished content. Useful for repurposing blog content that is too data-heavy for talking head.

6. **Data visualization / stats overlay** -- Animated charts or stats appearing on screen. Looks professional, works for the "state of" content. But production time is higher (even basic animations in CapCut take longer than a screen recording), and the format competes with much higher-production B2B content on these platforms.

### 2. Content Extraction Framework

For each blog post, extract 2-3 short-form video ideas using these four lenses.

**Lens 1: The Most Surprising Single Finding (30-second video)**
Pull the one data point or observation from the post that would make someone stop scrolling. This becomes the hook AND the entire video. No buildup needed. State the finding, show the evidence (screen recording or stat overlay), and end.

Example from Post 4 ("What ChatGPT Actually Says"):
- Finding: "I asked ChatGPT to name the best companies to work for in [industry]. Out of 200+ companies in that industry, it named the same 8 companies across almost every query."
- Format: Screen recording of the ChatGPT response, with Jordan's voiceover reacting to the pattern.

**Lens 2: The Most Quotable Stat (15-second hook video)**
A single statistic, delivered as text on screen or spoken to camera, designed to be watched and shared. No context needed -- the stat speaks for itself. Link in bio for the full post.

Example from Post 10 ("Your EVP Is Not Reaching AI"):
- Stat: "Companies spend $200K-$500K/year on employer brand. AI may not be reading any of it."
- Format: Text on screen, 3-second pause, then "Here is why" with a 12-second explanation.

**Lens 3: The "Most People Don't Know This" Angle (60-second explainer)**
Take the core argument of the post and distill it into a single insight that feels like insider knowledge. This is the "I work in this space and here is something nobody is talking about" format.

Example from Post 6 ("We Already Have a Strong Employer Brand"):
- Angle: "Having a strong employer brand and being visible to AI when candidates research employers are two completely different things. Here is why."
- Format: Talking head, 60 seconds, conversational delivery. End with "Ask AI about your company and see what happens."

**Lens 4: The "I Tested This" Angle (Screen recording)**
Run the experiment on camera. Ask AI a candidate-intent query, show the response, and narrate what it reveals. This is the most authentic format and the one most unique to this content niche.

Example from Post 7 ("How to Check Your Own AI Employer Visibility"):
- Test: Open ChatGPT on camera. Type "Should I work at [well-known company] or [competitor]?" Show the response. Point out what AI cites, what it misses, and what it gets wrong.
- Format: Screen recording with voiceover, 45-60 seconds.

### 4. Production Guide

#### Equipment
- iPhone (any model from the last 3 years). Rear camera for talking head, screen recording for demos.
- No ring light needed. Natural window light or a well-lit room is sufficient.
- No external microphone needed for the first batch. If audio quality is poor, a $20 lavalier mic that plugs into the iPhone is the maximum investment.
- Phone propped against books or a $10 tripod for talking head shots.

#### Recording Setup (Talking Head)
- Frame from chest up. No full body. No fancy background needed -- a clean wall, bookshelf, or home office works.
- Look at the camera lens, not the screen.
- Speak at a conversational pace, slightly faster than normal (short-form rewards energy).
- Record in vertical (9:16 aspect ratio). Always.
- Record each video 2-3 times. Pick the best take. Do not try to get it perfect in one shot.

#### Recording Setup (Screen Recording)
- Use the iPhone's built-in screen recording. Open ChatGPT in the mobile app or browser. Run the query in real time.
- Before recording: have the query typed out but not sent, so the video starts with the "send" action and the response appearing.
- Increase text size in the AI interface settings so it is legible on mobile.
- Record voiceover simultaneously (speak while recording the screen) or add it after in CapCut.

#### Editing (CapCut -- Free Version)
- Cut dead air at the beginning and end. The first frame should be the first word or the first action.
- Add auto-generated captions. This is non-negotiable -- 80%+ of viewers watch without sound.
- For stat overlay videos: use CapCut's text tool to place numbers on screen. Keep it simple. White text, dark background, bold font.
- For screen recordings: add a thin border/frame so it does not look like a raw screenshot.
- Total editing time per video: 5-10 minutes for talking head, 10-15 minutes for screen recordings with text overlay.

#### Batching Strategy
- Block two Sunday afternoons (April 20 and May 4) for recording.
- Session 1 (April 20): Record Videos 1-5. Change shirt between takes so they do not look like they were all recorded the same day.
- Session 2 (May 4): Record Videos 6-10.
- Edit in the 1-2 days after each recording session.
- Schedule posts in advance using TikTok's native scheduler and Instagram's native scheduler (both are free).

#### Posting Cadence
- Tuesday and Thursday, with an occasional Saturday.
- Post between 7-9 AM or 12-1 PM (these windows perform well on both platforms for professional content).
- Post to TikTok first, then upload the same video natively to Instagram Reels (do not cross-post via sharing -- upload the file separately so it does not carry the TikTok watermark).

### 5. Channel Strategy: Brand Identity Across Platforms

#### The Three-Channel Brand Map

| Channel | Voice | Identity | Can Name Antellion? | Tone |
|---------|-------|----------|---------------------|------|
| **LinkedIn** | Jordan Ellison, observer of AI + hiring trends | No company affiliation visible. Bio can say "Founder @ Antellion" and videos can reference the company. | Direct, energetic, slightly more casual than LinkedIn. "Here is what I found" energy. |
| **Blog (antellion.com)** | Antellion (company voice) | Company publication. Research-grade content. | YES -- it is the company site | Authoritative, evidence-based, enterprise-appropriate. No casualness. |

#### How the Channels Connect

The channels form a funnel:

1. **TikTok/Instagram** creates ambient awareness of the concept (AI employer visibility exists, it is measurable, your company may not be visible).
2. **LinkedIn** reinforces the concept through Jordan's personal credibility, but never names Antellion. A prospect who sees both channels may connect the dots, which is fine -- they just cannot find "Antellion" on Jordan's LinkedIn.
3. **Blog** is the credibility destination. "Link in bio" on TikTok/Instagram points to antellion.com. LinkedIn posts can link to blog posts framed in third person.
4. **Cold email** is the conversion mechanism. A prospect who has encountered the concept on any channel is warmer when the email arrives.

The key constraint: LinkedIn and TikTok/Instagram must never explicitly cross-reference each other. Jordan's LinkedIn does not mention TikTok videos. TikTok videos do not say "follow me on LinkedIn" (because the LinkedIn profile tells a different story). They operate in parallel, not in series.

### 6. Personal Brand vs. Company Account

**Recommendation: Personal account on both platforms. Not a company account.**

Here is why, specific to this situation:

**Arguments for personal account:**
- Personal accounts grow 3-5x faster than brand accounts on TikTok. The algorithm favors faces over logos.
- Instagram Reels from personal accounts get higher initial distribution than from business accounts.
- For B2B content, the "founder sharing insights" format is the most proven growth pattern on both platforms. Think: individual consultants and founders who built large followings by sharing niche expertise.
- The content is inherently personal -- "I ran these queries, I found this data, here is what I think it means." A company account posting this same content feels less authentic.
- Jordan is the credibility signal. For an early-stage company with no brand recognition, the human is more trustworthy than the logo.

**Arguments for company account:**
- Separates personal and professional identity.
- Could be handed off to a team member later.
- Looks more "professional" to enterprise buyers who find it.

**Why the personal arguments win:**
- There is no team to hand off to. Jordan is the team.
- Enterprise buyers who find Jordan's TikTok will not judge him for using a personal account. They will judge the content quality. A VP of TA who sees a sharp 45-second video about AI employer visibility does not care whether the account says "@jordanellison" or "@antellion." They care whether the insight is real.
- The growth advantage of personal accounts is significant when starting from zero. An @antellion TikTok account posting its first video will get near-zero distribution. @jordanellison posting the same video with a face in the thumbnail will get at least initial algorithmic testing.

**Account setup:**
- TikTok: @jordanellison (or @jordan.ellison if taken). Bio: "Researching how AI describes employers to candidates. Founder, Antellion." Link in bio: antellion.com
- Instagram: @jordanellison (same). Bio: same. Link in bio: same.
- Do NOT create separate @antellion accounts on either platform for now. If the content gains traction and Jordan wants to separate personal and company later, that migration can happen. Starting with two dead accounts (one personal, one company) is worse than starting with one active personal account.

### 7. Expanded Audience Reality Check

Returning to the audience question with more specificity.

**Who will actually see these videos?**

| Audience Segment | On TikTok? | On Instagram? | Value to Antellion |
|-----------------|------------|--------------|-------------------|
| VP Talent Acquisition (primary buyer) | Very unlikely in professional mode | Unlikely in professional mode | Direct buyer. Unreachable via short-form video. |
| Head of Employer Brand (primary buyer) | Unlikely | Possible (employer brand pros skew slightly younger and more digitally native) | Direct buyer. Marginally reachable on Instagram. |
| CPO / CHRO (primary buyer) | Very unlikely | Very unlikely | Direct buyer. Not reachable via short-form video. |
| Employer brand manager (practitioner) | Possible -- "HR TikTok" is a real niche | Likely -- this demographic is active on Instagram | Internal champion. Can surface the concept to their VP. Reachable. |
| Talent acquisition coordinator/recruiter (practitioner) | Yes -- recruiting content has a TikTok audience | Yes | Low decision-making power but can create internal awareness. |
| HR/talent content creators and journalists | Yes -- they monitor these platforms for trends | Yes | Amplifiers. Can write about the concept, cite the content, or invite Jordan as a guest. |
| "Future of work" generalist audience | Yes | Yes | Broad awareness. Shares content, creates social proof, but does not convert directly. |
| Other founders / B2B operators | Yes (startup TikTok is growing) | Yes | Potential referral sources, partnerships, or advisors. |

**Net assessment:**

Short-form video will not generate leads. It will generate awareness among a secondary audience that, over 3-6 months, creates conditions where the primary audience has heard of the concept before they receive a cold email. This is a long game, not a short game.

**The time math:**

- 10 videos over 30 days: ~3 hours of total production time
- 8 blog posts over 30 days: ~21 hours of total production time
- 14 LinkedIn posts over 30 days: ~5 hours of total production time

The video investment is small relative to the blog investment. If Jordan can genuinely batch 5 videos in 90 minutes, the marginal cost is low enough that it is worth testing even with uncertain audience reach. If it takes longer than that -- if each video becomes a 45-minute production cycle -- then it is not worth the time at this stage and should be deprioritized in favor of blog writing.

**The test:** Produce the first batch of 5 videos. Measure views, engagement, and (most importantly) whether any of the blog traffic or cold email engagement can be attributed to short-form video exposure. After 2 weeks, decide whether to continue. If total views across 5 videos are under 500, short-form video is not the channel for this content right now, and the second batch should be skipped.
