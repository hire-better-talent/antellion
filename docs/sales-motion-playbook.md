## How scripted should you be?

The answer is not "scripted vs. conversational." It is **scripted at the pivot, conversational everywhere else.**

A pivot is a 10-15 second moment on a call where the conversation transitions from *delivering the current thing* to *asking for the next thing.* These moments are where deals are won, lost, or stalled. Outside the pivots, the call should sound like a credible analyst walking a CHRO through findings — curious, specific, comfortable with silence. That tone is what differentiates Antellion from an SDR-driven vendor motion.

**Memorize word-perfect:**
- The pivot line (the 1-2 sentence ask)
- The #1 objection rebuttal for each pivot
- The BAMFAM close (the sentence that books the next meeting before you hang up)

**Keep conversational:**
- The delivery walkthrough itself
- Questions the buyer asks about findings
- Anything that touches their specific business context, competitors, or talent strategy
- Pricing explanations (once you have introduced the tiers)

**How to use this doc on a call.** Keep it open in a side window. The pivot lines and BAMFAM closes should be internalized so you can deliver them without glancing down. The rebuttals can be read if needed — buyers forgive a moment of "let me make sure I say this precisely" when the precision is about their money.

## The 4 pivot moments

### Transition 1 — Snapshot delivery → Diagnostic

**The moment.** You have walked the prospect through their Snapshot. They have seen the citation gap map, the top 5 Discovery queries where competitors appeared and they did not, and the shareable executive summary. They say something like "this is really useful," "I had no idea," or "what would you do with this?"

**The Hormozi mechanic.** This is the **Paid Attraction Offer** transition from *$100M Money Models* §2 (Attraction Offers). The free Snapshot generated the lead and the warmth; the Diagnostic is the first cash-generating step. The $4,900 price point is intentionally calibrated to a P-card or a simple PO — it bypasses the committee dynamic that kills $14K decisions. The "Win Your Money Back" guarantee is the **Grand Slam Offer risk reversal** from *$100M Offers* §5 (Value Equation — reducing perceived risk).

**Primary pivot line (memorize).**

> "What you are seeing in the Snapshot is the Discovery stage of how AI describes you — roughly a quarter of the real picture. The next thing I would do in your shoes is a Diagnostic: 40 queries, four models, three personas scoped to the roles you actually hire for, all four stages of the candidate journey. Ten business days, $4,900, and if we do not surface at least ten material findings you get the full fee back. If the findings warrant a deeper look, that $4,900 credits to the Baseline."

**Alternates (when the primary does not fit).**

*When the buyer is senior/skeptical and wants to know what is different from the Snapshot:*
> "The Snapshot is Discovery-heavy and deliberately light. The Diagnostic is scoped around the personas you actually recruit — so instead of a generic candidate voice, it is a Senior ML Engineer, a Staff Infra Engineer, and a VP Eng candidate asking the questions your candidates are actually typing. That is where the useful findings live."

*When the buyer asks "why not go straight to Baseline?":*
> "You can — and some people do. The Diagnostic exists because most CHROs want to see what the full-persona, full-journey output looks like before they authorize a two-week engagement. The $4,900 credits, so you are not paying twice if you move forward."

**#1 objection — "We already have an employer brand agency / careers site / recruitment marketing program. Why would I need this?"**

Rebuttal (memorize):

> "The Diagnostic does not replace any of that work. It tells the teams already doing that work which citations AI is currently drawing from and which it is missing — so the content your brand and recruitment marketing teams produce gets directed with more precision. Most employer brand programs are measured on candidate surveys and careers-site traffic. Neither signal tells you what AI says about you when a candidate asks Claude where to work. That is the gap the Diagnostic closes."

**BAMFAM close.**

> "I'll send you a scoping call invite for next week — 45 minutes, we confirm the three competitors and the job category, and the Diagnostic clock starts when the deposit clears. Does Tuesday or Thursday morning work better?"

**What NOT to say.**
- Do not mention Baseline pricing ($14K) at this pivot. The whole point of the Diagnostic is to bypass that procurement friction. Introducing the number now reanchors the conversation above the P-card line.
- Do not mention Phase 2 pricing. Premature.
- Do not mention the Board-Level Engagement. Especially premature.
- Do not say "it's a no-brainer" or any variant. Enterprise buyers audit that phrase and downgrade you.
- Do not offer to discount the $4,900. The refund guarantee *is* the risk reversal. Discounting signals you do not believe in the guarantee.

### Transition 2 — Diagnostic readout → Baseline

**The moment.** You have just walked the buyer through the Diagnostic Report and the executive Findings Brief. You have shown them 3-5 findings live in the interactive HTML view. Somewhere in the call they say "so what do we do with this?" or "what's the next level?" or — the strongest signal — they ask a question you cannot fully answer within the Diagnostic scope, like "which platforms specifically?" or "how do I sequence this?"

**The Hormozi mechanic.** This is the **Rollover commitment mechanic** from *$100M Money Models* §3 (Upsells), layered with **BAMFAM discipline** (Book A Meeting From A Meeting) also from §3. The rollover math is the single highest-leverage move in the entire motion: $4,900 rolls to $14K Baseline, net new spend is **$9,100**, which reframes the decision from "should I spend $14K?" to "should I spend an additional $9,100 to go three to four times deeper?"

**Primary pivot line (memorize).**

> "A couple of the findings I just walked you through — the citation monoculture on Perplexity and the persona invisibility gap — are at the edge of what the Diagnostic scope can show. The Baseline goes three to four times deeper: 300-400 queries, the full Journey Action Map, a prioritized remediation roadmap tied to named platforms. It's $14K, two weeks. Your $4,900 rolls forward — so the net is $9,100 — but only if we sign in the next 60 days."

**Alternates.**

*When the buyer is directly asking about next steps:*
> "The Baseline is the natural next step. Same methodology, three to four times the query volume, adds the Journey Action Map — which is the strategic scorecard that maps all four candidate stages to your #1 remediation priority per stage. $14K total, and your $4,900 credits, so you're looking at $9,100 net over two weeks."

*When the buyer is asking about implementation specifically:*
> "The Diagnostic tells you where the gaps are. The Baseline tells you which platform to start with, in what order, with which content type — that's the Journey Action Map. It's the deliverable your recruitment marketing and brand teams can actually run against. $9,100 net after your credit, delivered in two weeks."

**#1 objection — "Let me think about it / circulate this with the team first."**

Rebuttal (memorize):

> "Totally fair — circulate the Diagnostic, it's designed for exactly that. The one thing I'd flag is the 60-day credit window. If your team comes back in week eight saying 'let's go,' the $4,900 no longer rolls and the Baseline is $14K flat. So if you think there's real momentum here, the call I'd make is to send the Diagnostic out today and book the Baseline scoping for the week after, contingent on internal alignment. You can cancel the scoping call with 24 hours notice. What I'd hate is for the credit window to be the reason you pay more."

**BAMFAM close.**

> "Let me put the Baseline scoping call on the calendar for two Tuesdays from now — worst case we cancel, best case we've kept your credit window intact. I'll send the SOW and deposit invoice after the scoping call so nothing gets authorized before you're ready."

**What NOT to say.**
- Do not mention the Board-Level Engagement at this pivot. That belongs in Transition 3.
- Do not introduce Phase 2 pricing unless the buyer explicitly asks. The Baseline is the ask here. Keep the concentration.
- Do not treat the rollover expiration as a pressure tactic. State it as a fact about the contract and move on. If it sounds like urgency theater, you have lost credibility.
- Do not offer the Baseline + Action Brief here. That is a downsell for Phase 2 price resistance, not a Diagnostic-to-Baseline mechanic.
- Do not say "this is the best decision you can make." The findings speak for themselves; your job is to name the next step, not to sell it.

### Transition 3 — Baseline readout → Phase 2

**The moment.** You have just presented the Baseline executive readout — 60 minutes, the 40+ page report, the Journey Action Map, the 5+ prioritized remediation actions. The buyer is oriented to the scale of the problem. This is the most important pivot in the motion because it moves the deal from a five-figure engagement into a six-figure strategic initiative, and it is where procurement, CFO signoff, and budget cycle reality all enter the conversation.

**The Hormozi mechanic.** Three mechanics stacked:

1. **Anchor upsell** from *$100M Offers* §8 (price anchoring) — the $275K Board-Level Engagement is presented *first* to reset the frame before the three-tier menu.
2. **Menu upsell** from *$100M Money Models* §3 — Focused / Strategic / Enterprise is a true three-option menu where the buyer self-locates rather than being pushed.
3. **Rollover forward** from *$100M Money Models* §3 — the $14K Baseline credits into any Phase 2 engagement, so Focused is $75K net, Strategic is $90K net, Enterprise starts at $110K net.

**Primary pivot line (memorize).**

> "Given the scale of what the Baseline surfaced and the board-level narrative pressure you've described, I want to walk you through three levels at which we can engage in Phase 2. The top end is what we call a Board-Level Engagement — annual retainer, quarterly presentations at your offices, named senior analyst, ad-hoc re-scans on a fair-use basis. It runs $275,000 a year. Most customers at your scale land between our Strategic and Enterprise tiers, which I'll walk through after. But I want to show you the full architecture first."

*Then spend 5-7 minutes on Board-Level with genuine enthusiasm. Then move to Strategic and Enterprise.*

**Alternates.**

*When the buyer is clearly mid-market, not F500 (no board-level trigger):*
> "Based on the Baseline, the natural next step is our Focused Phase 2 — it adds three layers of conversational probing, the full citation ecosystem audit across your three competitors, and four quarterly re-scans over the next twelve months. Net cost after your Baseline credit is $75,000. Strategic and Enterprise scale that across more roles if you want broader coverage."

*When the buyer has already asked about pricing earlier in the call:*
> "As a reminder, Phase 2 sits on top of the Baseline you just paid for — so your $14K credits forward. Focused is $75K net, Strategic is $90K net, Enterprise starts at $110K. I want to walk through the scope differences, then tell you which one I'd pick if I were in your seat."

**#1 objection — "The budget for that would need to come from somewhere else / we can't do $89K this quarter."**

Rebuttal (memorize):

> "That's exactly the conversation I'd expect to have at this step. If the current-cycle budget is the constraint and not the methodology, there's a middle option I don't usually lead with — Baseline + Action Brief at $24K total. You've already paid $14K, so it's a $10K add for a prioritized remediation brief on the one journey stage you're most exposed on — at least 8 platform-specific recommendations and a 90-day execution sequence. That preserves your full $24K rollover credit forward to Phase 2 in a later quarter. It catches the budget-cycle gap without losing momentum."

**BAMFAM close.**

> "Let me put two things on the calendar — a Phase 2 scoping call for next week where we walk through which tier fits, and a hold on my delivery calendar for three weeks out so you're not fighting my schedule when you're ready. If the Phase 2 scope needs to shift to Action Brief for timing reasons, the delivery slot still works. Does Wednesday work for the scoping?"

**What NOT to say.**
- Do not lead with Baseline + Action Brief. It is a downsell, not the primary path. Leading with it anchors the buyer at $24K and collapses the deal into the discretionary-spend zone.
- Do not present Board-Level to a mid-market buyer who obviously does not fit the profile. Leading with a fake anchor on a $50M-revenue company damages trust. If you are not sure, ask: "Are you briefing your board on employer brand narrative, or is this a CHRO-to-CFO conversation?" The answer tells you which pivot to use.
- Do not apologize for the price. Present the numbers, stop talking, and let the buyer respond. Silence after a number is a sales skill; filling silence with justification is not.
- Do not offer Continuous at this pivot. Continuous is a Phase 2 close attachment, not a Phase 2 upsell — and it is not launched yet anyway.

### Transition 4 — Phase 2 close → Continuous attach *(GATED — post-launch only)*

**Launch gate.** Do not present Continuous until all three of these are true: (1) 2-3 Baselines delivered, (2) monitoring alert logic built in the product, (3) at least one Phase 2 customer willing to be a Continuous reference. Until then, leave the Phase 2 close uncluttered. Offering a subscription you cannot service is worse than not offering one. Target launch: **June 1, 2026.**

**The moment (once launched).** The Phase 2 SOW is ready to sign. The buyer has reviewed the scope, the guarantee, the timeline. This is the moment of peak trust — you have delivered the Baseline, you have proven the methodology, and the buyer is about to commit to a six-figure engagement. Per Hormozi's continuity research (*$100M Money Models* §4), this is the single highest-attach moment in the entire relationship.

**The Hormozi mechanic.** Three mechanics:

1. **Continuity at peak trust** from *$100M Money Models* §4 — present on the same signature page, not reopened later.
2. **Waived Fee** from *$100M Money Models* §4 — $25K activation fee waived with 12-month commitment.
3. **Continuity Discount** from *$100M Money Models* §4 — first quarterly re-scan (~$13K value) included when signed alongside Phase 2. This is the 2x-ratio mechanic that drives attach.

**Primary pivot line (memorize).**

> "One last thing before we countersign. Most Phase 2 customers add Continuous at signature — it's a monthly monitoring layer that handles quarterly re-scans, narrative drift alerts, and a priority question channel between deep engagements. $6,500 a month, 12-month term. Because you're signing it alongside Phase 2, the $25K activation fee is waived and your first quarterly re-scan is included at no charge. The logic is that the Phase 2 work stands up the baseline; Continuous is what tells you whether AI's story about you is actually changing in response."

**Alternates.**

*When the buyer is cautious/wants to think:*
> "The one reason to decide now rather than in 90 days is the activation fee waiver and the first re-scan credit — those are attached to same-signature-page execution, not to Continuous standalone. You can cancel month-13 with 60 days notice, no penalty. If you want the monitoring layer eventually, signing it now saves about $38K in Year 1."

*When the buyer asks what Continuous does that Phase 2 re-scans do not:*
> "Phase 2 includes four quarterly re-scans over twelve months. Continuous adds the monthly monitoring layer between those scans — sentiment flips, citation drift, new competitor mentions, hallucinations. Quarterly gives you trajectory; monthly alerts catch the things you'd want to know about the week they happen, not the quarter they happen."

**#1 objection — "Let's get through Phase 2 first and revisit."**

Rebuttal (memorize):

> "Completely reasonable — and if Phase 2 delivers what you expect, we'll have the conversation again. The one caveat is that the waiver and the first-re-scan credit don't come back on a follow-up pitch. So the question is whether you're 80%+ likely to want monitoring eventually. If yes, signing now saves $38K; if no, Phase 2 stands alone and you haven't lost anything. I'm not going to push on this — it's your call."

**BAMFAM close.**

> "Here's what I'll do. I'll send you the Phase 2 SOW to sign today, and I'll include the Continuous addendum as an optional second page. You can initial it or not — no pressure, same invoice either way. If you want to talk through Continuous specifically, let me put 20 minutes on the calendar for Friday."

**What NOT to say.**
- Do not present Continuous before you have all three launch gates cleared.
- Do not bundle Continuous into Phase 2 pricing. It must be a separate, optional signature block.
- Do not imply penalty for declining. The waiver is an incentive for signing now, not a punishment for signing later.
- Do not talk about Year 2 economics ("this pays for itself," etc.). Enterprise buyers do not reason that way about monitoring spend.

## The rollover clock as a sales tool

The 60-day rollover expiration is a genuine contract term, not a pressure tactic. But it is the most powerful commitment mechanic in the entire motion (*$100M Money Models* §3 — time-bound decoys). Used correctly it creates urgency without sounding like urgency theater.

**How to weave it in:**

- **At the Diagnostic scoping call:** "The $4,900 credits to Baseline if you sign within 60 days of Diagnostic delivery. That's written into the SOW — I mention it so it's not a surprise."
- **At the Diagnostic readout:** "Your 60-day credit window opens today, closes [specific date]. I don't bring this up to push — I bring it up because I'd rather you know it than miss it."
- **At the Baseline readout:** "Same structure — $14K rolls into Phase 2 within 60 days of today's readout. The date is [specific date]. If you need more time, the rollover lapses but the engagement doesn't change."
- **In follow-up emails:** "Heads up — your credit window closes [date]. Not pushing, just marking the calendar."

**What makes this enterprise-appropriate vs. carnival:** state the date, state that you are mentioning it so they know, and move on. Do not reference "act now," "limited time," "special offer." The rollover is a contract term — treat it like one.

**Why it works.** CHROs and CPOs have discretionary budget; discretionary budget gets redeployed constantly. A 60-day window forces the budget conversation to happen inside the quarter instead of sliding into "next quarter" forever. The buyer who wants to move forward is grateful you named the date. The buyer who does not want to move forward was not going to anyway.

## Nurture cadence between pivots

For prospects who do not convert at the first pivot. Pulled from *$100M Leads* Book IV (nurture) with timing and content adapted for enterprise buyers.

### After Snapshot delivered, no Diagnostic booked

- **Day 3:** Reply-able email. One paragraph. Reference one specific finding from their Snapshot ("the gap on Glassdoor for ML roles specifically"). No ask. Signal: "I noticed this, thought you should see it."
- **Day 7:** Share a related finding from a separate engagement (anonymized). "I saw a similar citation monoculture pattern on a customer in [adjacent industry] — they solved it by [specific action]. Happy to walk you through what they did."
- **Day 14:** Offer the Diagnostic scoping call directly with a new hook — "I have time Thursday if the top-5 Discovery gaps are worth 30 minutes."
- **Day 30:** Short check-in with a new piece of content — a blog post, a framework piece, a competitive observation. No calendar ask.
- **Day 60:** Final direct ask. "The Diagnostic still makes sense for [companyName] — 10 business days, $4,900, refund if we don't hit 10 findings. I'll stop pinging after this if the timing isn't right."
- **Day 90:** Quarterly check-in. New finding, new data, no ask.

### After Diagnostic delivered, no Baseline booked

- **Day 3:** Send them a specific additional observation you held back from the Diagnostic — something you noticed but did not make the report. Signals continued analytical attention.
- **Day 14:** Case-study-style note — "Had a Baseline readout this week where the Journey Action Map surfaced [specific pattern]. Made me think of your Consideration-stage finding. Worth the two weeks if you have budget cycle room."
- **Day 30:** Reminder of the rollover window with a specific date. One sentence. No pitch.
- **Day 45:** Last substantive touch before the window closes. "Credit window closes [date]. Want to jump on a scoping call this week or next?"
- **Day 60:** Window closed. Short note acknowledging the lapse. Keep the door open: "If Baseline still makes sense, pricing is $14K flat. I'll reach out in 90 days with a new observation."

### After Baseline delivered, no Phase 2 booked

- **Day 5:** Send the first "new finding" — something that has shifted in their AI narrative since the Baseline delivered. Specific, evidence-based.
- **Day 14:** Industry observation. "I just finished a Focused engagement in your peer set. One pattern: [specific finding]. You'd see the same in your data if you ran the multi-layer pass."
- **Day 30:** Explicit Phase 2 check-in. Reference the Journey Action Map priorities from their Baseline. "Where did remediation on [priority 1] land?"
- **Day 45:** Offer the Action Brief downsell if they have not already pushed back on Phase 2 price.
- **Day 60:** Window closed. One note. "Credit window closed, Phase 2 is flat pricing now. I'll send quarterly observations — if something changes, I'm a reply away."

### Principle

Every touch in the cadence must carry *new information about the prospect* (a finding, an observation, a pattern). "Just checking in" is banned. You are an analyst, not an SDR — your follow-up is justified by the work, not the schedule.

## When to go off-script entirely

Enterprise buyers are quietly testing whether you are a vendor or a consultant. The test is whether you hold your script when the conversation goes somewhere interesting. Holding the script is how you lose the deal.

**Abandon the pivot when:**

1. **The buyer names a trigger event.** M&A, activist investor pressure, CHRO transition, reputation crisis, hiring sprint announcement, board-level narrative scrutiny. These are Board-Level or Enterprise signals. Drop the scripted pivot, ask deep questions about the trigger, and let the engagement scope emerge from their answer.
2. **The buyer reveals budget authority directly.** "I can approve up to $100K without committee." "Our board approved $2M for talent transformation this year." This is a self-located buyer — they have already done the pivot work for you. Skip to the scoping question: "Given that authority, what's the scope you want to cover?"
3. **The buyer asks a question your tier menu cannot answer.** "Can you do this in Japanese?" "Can you measure how AI describes our private equity ownership?" "Can you run this against an acquisition target?" These are custom-scope conversations. Stop presenting the menu and start asking what success looks like.
4. **The buyer signals discomfort with pace.** "This feels fast." "I want to slow down." Drop the BAMFAM close and explicitly give them room. "Let's not book the next call today. Read the report, share it internally, and email me when you want to talk." Enterprise buyers reward this; it is the single most counterintuitive sales move and it works.
5. **The buyer starts diagnosing their own problem out loud.** When a CHRO starts saying "we have a real issue with how we show up for mid-career engineers, and I think it traces back to our last leadership change" — stop talking. Let them keep going. Ask clarifying questions. The scope of the engagement is being drafted by the buyer in real time. Your job is to not interrupt it.
6. **The conversation turns to someone else's opinion.** "My CFO is going to want to see X." "My board will ask about Y." This is procurement thinking out loud. Pivot scripts are for the individual; now the conversation is about committee. Ask: "What would the CFO need to see to approve this?" and listen. The answer tells you what needs to be in the proposal.

**The signal you are still on the right track:** the buyer is asking you questions. The signal you have drifted: you are asking the buyer questions and getting short answers.

## Summary — the four lines to memorize

If you memorize nothing else, memorize these and their BAMFAM closes:

1. **Snapshot → Diagnostic:** "What you are seeing in the Snapshot is the Discovery stage — roughly a quarter of the real picture. The next thing I would do in your shoes is a Diagnostic: 40 queries, four models, three personas, all four stages. $4,900, refund if we don't hit 10 findings, credits to Baseline if you go deeper."

2. **Diagnostic → Baseline:** "A couple of the findings I just walked through are at the edge of what the Diagnostic can show. The Baseline goes three to four times deeper — 300-400 queries, the Journey Action Map, a prioritized roadmap. $14K total, your $4,900 rolls, so the net is $9,100 — within 60 days."

3. **Baseline → Phase 2:** "Given the scale of what the Baseline surfaced, I want to walk you through three levels at which we can engage. The top end — Board-Level Engagement — runs $275K a year. Most customers at your scale land between our Strategic and Enterprise tiers. I'll walk through the full architecture first."

4. **Phase 2 → Continuous (post-launch):** "Most Phase 2 customers add Continuous at signature — monthly monitoring, quarterly re-scans, priority question channel. $6,500/month. Because you're signing it alongside Phase 2, the $25K activation is waived and your first re-scan is free. Everything else is analyst conversation.
