## Post #15 — Tuesday, May 12

**Source:** Blog Post 12 (methodology) — first pull
**Format:** Framework
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01 (no anti-audit framing), RULE-02 (no overclaim), RULE-10 (sector-neutral), RULE-11 (no acronyms), RULE-20 (no URL), RULE-21 (punch-line close)

---

An AI employer visibility scan isn't one query.

It's 40 candidate-intent queries, across 4 AI models, across 3 candidate personas, across 4 candidate-journey stages.

40 × 4 × 3 = 480 model responses per company. That's the unit of work for one company. That's what you compare to peer companies. That's what the chief HR officer looks at when they ask whether candidates are forming a coherent or fragmented impression of the company across the AI surface.

Why each dimension exists:

— **40 queries** is the floor where the variance in any single AI answer stops dominating the signal. Below 10 per stage, you measure noise. Above 40, returns diminish — the seventh culture question doesn't surface anything the third one didn't.

— **4 AI models** because the same question to ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity produces meaningfully different answers. Citation sources, training cutoffs, sentiment synthesis all diverge across models. The divergence itself is one of the most actionable findings.

— **3 candidate personas** because AI doesn't produce the same answer for "your company as an employer" that it produces for "your company as an employer to a senior AE candidate" or "to a staff engineer candidate" or "to a senior CSM candidate." The persona conditions the synthesis. Different answers.

— **4 candidate-journey stages** — Discovery, Consideration, Evaluation, Commitment — because a company can be visible at one stage and structurally absent at another, and the remediation for each pattern is different.

The 480 figure isn't marketing. It's what survives once each dimension has enough coverage that the finding is reproducible rather than anecdotal.

Hiring-specific measurement is structured this way for the same reason any other measurement instrument is structured: the dimensions are not optional, and the question you can answer is bounded by the dimensions you actually measured.

## Post #16 — Wednesday, May 13

**Source:** Blog Post 12 (methodology) — second pull
**Format:** Data Point
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01, RULE-02, RULE-10 (rotated example: financial services), RULE-11 (no acronyms), RULE-20, RULE-21

---

Same query, asked of ChatGPT and Perplexity yesterday: "What are the best companies in commercial banking to work for as a senior banker?"

Three of the seven companies named overlapped between the two models.

The other four were different. Different on each model.

That's a sample of one query, across two of the four AI systems candidates are using when they research employers. Three more queries on the same persona would produce a wider variation map. A 40-query batch would produce a stable signal — which companies AI tends to name, which it never names, and where the named set diverges meaningfully across the models.

A scan run against a single AI model is doing something useful, but it isn't producing a measurement that generalizes. It's producing one answer from one synthesis, treated as if it were *the* answer.

The signal is the variance across the models, not the answer from any one of them.

Two implications:

The first: when a candidate tells you they "asked ChatGPT" and got a specific impression of the company, the candidate down the hall who asked Claude or Perplexity may have gotten a different impression. Both impressions affect whether the recruiter ping gets a response. The recruiting team is operating in a multi-model citation environment whether or not anyone has formally measured it.

The second: the sentiment-divergence pattern across the four models is one of the highest-signal findings in a hiring-specific audit. It tells you which citation source is producing the disagreement, and whether the disagreement is going to widen or close on its own. Most of the time, the disagreement closes when one specific stale citation gets refreshed somewhere on the public web. Knowing which one is the action.

One AI scan, one model: a sample of one. Four models, structured queries, persona-coded: a measurement.

## Post #17 — Friday, May 15

**Source:** Blog Post 13 (Senior AE persona) — primary pull
**Format:** Data Point
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01, RULE-02, RULE-10 (sector-rotated worked examples in source post), RULE-11 (no acronyms), RULE-20, RULE-21

---

I ran a Senior AE query batch across three sample companies last week. 30 candidate-intent queries per company, across the four AI models — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity. Same persona across all three.

The pattern that surprised me:

AI described the commission structure *differently for the same company across three of the four models.*

The variance across models was wider than the variance across companies.

A senior account executive researching one of the three companies, using ChatGPT or Claude, came away with a specific compensation expectation — named base-variable split, named accelerator philosophy, a number range cited from one of the public sales-compensation surfaces. The same candidate using Gemini received a generic answer with no number range. The same candidate using Perplexity received a third version, citing a different source.

Three different impressions of the same compensation plan, depending on which AI tool the candidate happened to consult.

The candidate isn't doing anything wrong. They're using the AI that loads when they ask the question. The variance is structural — different training cutoffs, different citation surfaces, different synthesis behavior across the four models.

What this means for the recruiting team:

Candidates who pass on a recruiter outreach because the AI gave them a thin or unflattering compensation picture aren't telling anyone. The conversation never happens. The recruiter sees no signal. The funnel leak is invisible to the applicant tracking system because it occurred before the system would have logged anything.

What it looks like to the chief HR officer:

Pipeline volume varies in a pattern that doesn't map cleanly to recruiter activity or to the careers site funnel. The unmeasured input is the AI synthesis the candidate consulted before responding to the recruiter ping.

Persona-coded measurement is what makes this addressable. A single-persona, single-model scan would have flagged none of it.
