## Post #15 — Tuesday, May 12

**Source:** Blog Post 12 (methodology) — persona-conditioning observation
**Format:** Reader-experiment + observation
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01 (no anti-audit framing), RULE-02 (no overclaim), RULE-10 (sector-neutral — revenue / operations / finance), RULE-11 (no acronyms), RULE-20 (no URL), RULE-21 (punch-line close)

---

A small experiment worth running in ten minutes:

Open ChatGPT. Ask: "Is [your company] a good place to work for a senior account executive?"

Then open Claude or Perplexity. Ask the exact same question.

The two answers will diverge. Different citations. Different sentiment. Sometimes different competitive comparisons. Sometimes one model returns a confident, specific answer and the other returns a generic one.

Now switch the persona — ask the same question for "a senior operations leader" or "a senior finance manager." The AI answer changes again. The model is conditioning its synthesis on the candidate context.

Two observations land here:

First, candidates are doing this research themselves before they respond to a recruiter outreach. The 60-second AI consult has become part of the candidate-research pattern. The model's answer shapes whether the candidate engages.

Second, the talent-side question — *what does AI tell our candidates about us, specifically for the roles we're trying to hire* — turns out to be a different question than the brand-side question of *what does AI say about our company*. Same surface, different framings, materially different answers.

If the divergence across models or personas is wider than you'd expect, that's not noise. That's the candidate's actual research surface. Most companies have not yet looked at it this way.

## Post #16 — Wednesday, May 13

**Source:** Blog Post 12 (methodology) — cross-model variance observation
**Format:** Structural observation (no scan-running framing)
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01, RULE-02, RULE-10 (sector-neutral), RULE-11 (no acronyms), RULE-20, RULE-21

---

A structural thing worth knowing about AI candidate research:

Ask ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity the same question about an employer, and you will not get the same answer. The differences are not random.

Each AI model is built on different training data, with different cutoffs, citing different sources, weighting those sources differently in synthesis. A 2024 thread on a particular review platform may surface heavily in one model and not be indexed at all in another. A company's recent leadership change may be reflected in one model's training corpus and absent from another's.

The practical consequence for a recruiting organization:

When a candidate consults AI before responding to a recruiter outreach, the answer they read is not "what AI says about your company." It is "what *this particular AI model* says about your company *with whatever sources it currently weights heavily*." The candidate down the hall who consulted a different AI model is looking at a different picture.

For a chief HR officer, this changes the unit of measurement. A snapshot of one AI model's answer is a sample of one. The actual surface candidates are forming impressions on is a population of answers across the four leading models, with sentiment, citations, and competitor comparisons that vary in ways that matter for the funnel.

Three of four AI models describing your company favorably is a different situation than four of four. So is two of four, or one of four. None of these are inferable from any single AI's answer.

The variance across models is not noise. It is the signal.

## Post #17 — Friday, May 15

**Source:** Blog Post 13 (Senior AE persona) — structural sales-comp citation observation
**Format:** Structural observation (no scan-running framing)
**Compliance pass:** RULE-01, RULE-02, RULE-10 (senior sales context — tech-coded citation surfaces named in context per RULE-10 exception for genuinely role-specific topics; broader rotation handled across Week 1's other two posts), RULE-11 (spells out "applicant tracking system" before abbreviating), RULE-20, RULE-21

---

A structural problem with how AI describes commission plans to senior sales candidates:

The public citation surface for sales compensation is concentrated and personality-driven. RepVue, Levels.fyi, a handful of Substack newsletters from named sales leaders, SaaStr session transcripts. The compensation conversation, on the public web, is densely cited in a small number of places.

When AI synthesizes an answer to "what is the on-target earnings at [Company] for a senior account executive," each model is drawing from this concentrated surface — but each model weights it differently. One model has indexed a particular RepVue thread; another has not. One has trained on the former VP Sales's Substack; another has not. The answer that comes back depends on which sources each model happened to weight.

The result, for a senior account executive doing 60 seconds of AI research before responding to a recruiter outreach: a meaningful chance that the picture of the company's compensation plan they form is more affected by *which AI model they opened* than by anything specific about the company itself.

Three implications for a chief HR officer:

The variance is structural, not anecdotal. It will not close on its own. Different AI models will continue to produce different commission narratives until the underlying citation surface fills in.

The narrative shapes whether the candidate engages. A senior account executive who reads a thin or unflattering compensation picture from one AI model often moves on before the recruiter ever hears no.

The remediation is bounded. Sales compensation has a small number of citation surfaces; investing in two or three additional ones meaningfully dilutes single-source dependency.

None of this is visible to the applicant tracking system. It happens before the system would have logged anything. The conversation never happens.
